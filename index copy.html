The Promise of the MOOC Revolution

In the early 2010s, a bold promise swept through education: world-class courses from MIT, Harvard, and Stanford free, online, and open to anyone. Platforms like Coursera and edX pledged to democratise learning, letting a student in rural Kenya access the same material as one in Cambridge.

But behind the headlines of millions of enrolments, what does the data actually tell us? Did MOOCs deliver on their revolutionary promise, or did old inequalities simply follow learners online?

Sign-Up Is the Easy Part

Now let's zoom into Harvard and MIT MOOC data specifically. Every year, hundreds of thousands of learners enrol, drawn by the prospect of an Ivy League education at no cost.

But enrolment is just a click. The harder question is: who actually stays? Does the MOOC revolution serve the ambitious high-school graduate, or does it mostly benefit learners who are already highly educated, people who arguably needed it least?

The Leaky Funnel

Visualising learner journeys as a funnel reveals a brutal truth. The widest point is day zero, the moment someone clicks "Enrol". From there, the funnel narrows with alarming speed. Many learners never watch a single lecture. A large portion disengage within the first week.

This isn't unique to Harvard or MIT — it's a near-universal pattern across online learning. But the shape of the funnel changes depending on who you are. Use the filters below to explore how education background and gender affect how far learners make it through the course.

Key insight: The steepest drop-offs occur within the first few days of enrolment. Learners who remain active beyond day 10 are far more likely to complete the course entirely.

A Revolution... But for Whom?

The data tells a nuanced story. MOOCs have undeniably democratised access to elite education, but access alone isn't enough. The steepest drop-offs consistently hit learners with no post-secondary education, the very group the revolution promised to lift. Those already holding degrees engage longer and complete at higher rates.

Gender, however, paints a more encouraging picture. Women who enrol persist at similar rates to men. The gap isn't in completion, it's in who feels invited to sign up in the first place, particularly in technical subjects.

The MOOC revolution widened the door. But sustaining engagement for first-generation learners, those with the most to gain, remains the unsolved challenge of online education.
